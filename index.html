Abstract

Compositional generalizationâ€”the ability to recombine known factors into novel configurationsâ€”is essential for reliable image and video generation. We conduct controlled experiments that vary (i) the distribution type optimized by the training objective (continuous vs. categorical), and (ii) the information content of the conditioning signal during training (full, quantized, or partially missing). Our findings are consistent across datasets and modalities: continuous objectives (DiT, MAR, GIVT) yield robust Level-2 compositionality; categorical objectives (MaskGIT) struggle. Full, precise conditioning during training is critical; a JEPA-style auxiliary loss improves discrete models.

Findings

Finding 1 â€” Continuous vs. Categorical Objectives

Generative models trained to model continuous distributions reliably exhibit strong compositionality, whereas models trained on discrete, categorical distributions do not. Tokenizer choice (VAE vs. VQ-VAE) is not the decisive factor.

Finding 2 â€” Importance of Full Conditioning Information

Compositional generalization decreases when the conditioning signal is quantized or incomplete, even if all factors are provided during generation.

Less precise or missing labels during training impair recombination later.

Finding 3 â€” JEPA Auxiliary Objective Helps Discrete Models

Augmenting categorical models (e.g., MaskGIT) with a JEPA-style continuous objective improves compositional generalization while preserving discrete sampling advantages.
